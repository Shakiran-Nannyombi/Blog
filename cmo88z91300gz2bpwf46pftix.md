I recently built my first mobile MVP using React Native.

It was one of those moments you look forward to as a developer. New stack, real project, actual pressure.

We’re preparing for a startup pitch, and I’m the one responsible for bringing the idea to life. I hadn’t done mobile development before with React Native, but that didn’t matter. I figured it out as I went.

And when I finally had something working… I was proud of it.

**Not perfect, but real.**

So I shared it with the team.

And then the feedback started coming in 😬.

At first, it was small things. Suggestions. Adjustments. Then more comments. Then more.

Before I even realized it, I had mentally checked out.

Instead of engaging with the feedback, I handed it off to a junior teammate to “handle.” On the surface, it looked like a delegation. Leadership, even.

But if I’m being honest, it wasn’t.

## What I Was Actually Feeling

I just didn’t want to deal with it.

I told myself I don’t like being told what to do. That people were being too critical. That they didn’t understand how much effort I had put in.

But underneath all of that… I was just tired.

And something else I didn’t want to admit: I felt unappreciated.

Later that day, I stepped away from my laptop.

No code. No Slack. Just distance.

And in that quiet moment, something uncomfortable surfaced.

This wasn’t new.

### **<mark class="bg-yellow-200 dark:bg-yellow-500/30">The Pattern I Couldn’t Ignore</mark>**

I’ve done this before.

There were times I chose to work alone just to avoid feedback. Times I ignored input because it felt easier to stay in control. I even lost a client once because feedback became too overwhelming, and instead of handling it, I pulled away.

That pattern had been following me longer than I realized.

And for the first time, I stopped blaming the situation and asked a different question:

> *What if the problem isn’t the feedback?*

When I came back later that evening, everything felt different.

The same comments that annoyed me earlier suddenly made sense. Some of them were actually good. Necessary, even.

Nothing had changed about the feedback.

But something had changed in me.

That’s when it clicked:

> **The problem wasn’t the feedback. It was my state when I received it.**

## Why Developers Struggle With Feedback

As developers, we like to think we’re logical. That we evaluate things based on facts, data, and reasoning.

But feedback doesn’t just hit your logic, it hits your psychology.

In [Thanks for the Feedback](https://books.google.co.ug/books/about/Thanks_for_the_Feedback.html?id=Da_KAQAAQBAJ&redir_esc=y), the authors explain that we don’t reject feedback because it’s wrong; we reject it because of how it makes us feel.

Sometimes we think the feedback itself is inaccurate, so we dismiss it entirely. Other times, we reject it because of *who* is giving it. And in some cases, it goes deeper; it threatens how we see ourselves.

That last one is the hardest.

> Because once feedback touches your identity, it stops being about code and starts feeling personal.

Looking back, I can see exactly what happened.

*   My ego wanted recognition.
    
*   My exhaustion made me reactive.
    
*   And my sense of ownership made the code feel like an extension of myself.
    

So when the feedback came in, I didn’t process it; I reacted to it.

In [Mindset: The New Psychology of Success](https://share.google/4B2ue2h82C7X3gmOQ), this is the difference between a fixed mindset and a growth mindset. In a fixed mindset, feedback feels like judgment. In a growth mindset, it becomes information.

That shift sounds simple.

**But in practice, it’s hard, especially when you’re tired and emotionally invested.**

What changed for me wasn’t a new tool or technique.

*   It was learning to pause.
    
*   To step away before responding.
    
*   To come back when my mind was clear enough to actually *process* what was being said.
    

**Because there’s a big difference between reacting to feedback and processing it.**

| Reacting is | Processing is |
| --- | --- |
| Instant. | Slower. |
| Defensive. | Curious. |
| Emotional. | Intentional. |

And that difference changes everything.

## My Feedback Framework (What I Do Now)

Since then, I’ve started approaching feedback differently.

I check my state before I respond. If I’m exhausted or irritated, I don’t trust my first reaction.

I remind myself that my code is not my identity. It’s just something I built, and like anything built, it can be improved.

Even when feedback feels off, I try to find the small part of it that’s useful. There’s almost always something there.

And most importantly, I don’t run from it anymore.

Because avoiding feedback doesn’t protect you, it limits you.

### What Senior Engineers Do Differently

There’s something I’ve started noticing about more experienced engineers.

They don’t just tolerate feedback.

They look for it.

They ask questions. They dig deeper. They don’t try to prove they’re right—they try to get better.

And that’s a very different mindset.

If I’m being completely honest, this is still something I’m working on.

I still feel that resistance sometimes. That small voice that wants to defend, ignore, or step away.

But now I recognize it for what it is.

> **Not truth. Just a reaction.**

So if you’re building something, and the feedback starts coming in, especially when you’ve put a lot into it, pause for a second.

Ask yourself:

***Am I reacting… or processing?***

Because that one question might change how you grow as a developer.
